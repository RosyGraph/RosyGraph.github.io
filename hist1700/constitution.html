Groups for and against Ratification
  • Federalists
    • For ratification
    • Older demographic
    • Concerns
      • Foreign domination
      • Economic stagnation due to weak credit
      • Debt forgiveness
      • The "mob"
  • Anti-Federalists
    • Against ratification
    • Concerns
      • National power to "pick winners"

Constitution Creates a Single Sovereign Nation State
  • Federal Government
    • Powers over
    • Currency
      • By civil war all money was legal tender
      • Transition from precious metal economy
    • External commerce
    • Internal taxation and interstate commerce
    • Defense and diplomacy
      • President can ask congress for war
      • Congress must approve
    • Will honor national and state war debt
    • Will not pass laws cancelling private debt
    • Determines bankruptcy laws for all states
  • Alexander Hamilton
    • Rolls state bonds over to national bonds
    • Interest rate becomes fixed
    • Promises to pay off bonds in 15 years
    • Introduces tariffs to pay for bonds
Congressional Rules Not Specified

    • House has the power to not seat congressman
Presidential Veto Power
  • Unthinkable in the Patriots' vision of 1775
  • Common sense after the executiveless Revolution
  • Can be overridden with 2/3 majority
  • Before Civil War, presidents only vetoed unconstitutional laws
  • Now presidents veto laws they politically disagree with
Article VI

“This Constitution, and the Laws of the United States which shall be made in Pursuance thereof... Shall be the supreme Law of the Land; and the Judges in every State shall be bound thereby, any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any State... notwithstanding"

Article I Section 8 - The Interstate Commerce Clause

"The Congress shall have power to lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises, to pay the Debts and provide for the common Defense and general Welfare of the United States… To borrow Money on the credit of the United States; To regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several states… To establish… uniform laws on the subject of Bankruptcies throughout the United States.”

  • In national emergency, tariffs may be passed by executive
  • If congress gives up power, executive may be able to take it
Article I Section 8 - The Escalator Clause
  • [The Congress shall have the power] “To make all Laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into Execution the foregoing Powers, and all other Powers vested by this Constitution in the Government of the United States, or in any Department or Officer thereof."
  • State's rights question - why necessary?
  • Compare to the 10th Amendment
Article I Section 9 - The Suspension Clause
  • “The Privilege of the Writ of Habeas Corpus shall not be suspended, unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it.”
  • Clause did not prevent internment of Japanese Americans in 1942
The Bill of Rights
  • The result of anti-Federalist arguments during the debate over Constitutional ratification
  • Not the title of a single document, instead
  • It consists of the first 10 amendments to the U.S. Constitution, which were ratified in 1791
  • 19 amendments were proposed, 10 were ratified
  • The Bill of Rights marks America’s first contribution to what will develop into “Human Rights” theory in the late 20 th century
    • The best argument against ratification - did not discuss human rights
    • Federalists promised to cover this - thus BoR was born
The First Amendment
  • “Congress shall make no law”
    • Creating an established (state) religion
    • Limiting the “free exercise” of religion
    • Limiting free speech or a free press
      • Justice Holmes - shouting fire in a crowded room not protected
      • Incitements for violence are not protected
    • Limiting freedom of assembly
      • If public safety is threatened this may change
      • i.e. Neo-Nazi protest in Illinois
    • Limiting petitions to governmental officials for the “redress of grievances”
The Second Amendment
  • “A well regulated militia, being necessary to the security of a free state, the right of the people to keep and bear arms, shall not be infringed.”
  • Much argument about the meaning of the Second Amendment in the 20th Century
  • Current consensus: Congress can reasonably regulate the ownership of various types of firearms, but can not ban them entirely from private (non-“militiaman”) citizen possession
  • National Firearms Act of 1934
    • Enumerated types of illegal firearms
      • Grenade launchers
      • Silencers
      • Anti-tank
    • Required a tax and registration for automatic firearms
The Fourth Amendment
  • No unreasonable searches of private property
  • No unreasonable seizures of private property (links with the “just compensation” clause of the Fifth Amendment
  • Arrest and search warrants can only be issued on probable cause
  • Heavily litigated by civil libertarians in the late 20th century to restrain powers of local police
The Fifth Amendment
  • No capital trial (outside the military) without a grand jury indictment
  • No double jeopardy in criminal trials
  • No obligation to be a “witness against oneself” (intended to prevent torture of suspects)
  • No deprivation of life, liberty, or property without due process of law
  • No private property seizures for public use without just compensation
  • Miranda Rights
The Sixth Amendment
  • In Criminal Trials, the accused has a right to
    • A “speedy and public” trial
    • An impartial jury
    • To know the charges against him
    • To confront the witnesses against him
    • To compel witnesses to testify on his behalf
    • To have the assistance of counsel (but no absolute right to an attorney until the 1960s)
