The Trans-Mississippi West

National Developments 1865-1877

  • 1865 - The Civil War ends
    • Southern Confederacy is defeated, slavery ends, and the Union is preserved
    • Former slaves do NOT receive permanent civil rights
  • 1865-1877
    • Reconstruction
    • South preserves White Supremacy at the cost of 80 years of Southern economic underdevelopment (the “Jim Crow Era” after the 1890s)
    • End of first half of US History
  • 1862-1863 - Congress and President Lincoln implement legislation to aid in the development of the “Trans-Mississippi West”
  • 1863-1869 - The first transcontinental railroad (Central U.S.), followed by Northern and Southern TCRRs (finished 1883)
  • 1873-1877 - A depression briefly slows the American economy

Reconstruction

  • Period immediately following the American Civil War
  • A failed attempt to provide permanent political rights for freed slaves
  • Defeated by a combination of
    • Protracted violent resistance of Southerners (“the Klan”)
    • Gradual waning of Northern states’ commitment to ensure equal political rights for the “freedmen”
  • Reconstruction produces three Constitutional Amendments
    • 13th , 14th , 15th Amendments - not enforced by Federal Government
    • Each of these were neutralized during the “Jim Crow Era” from the 1890s, preserving White Supremacy in the South until 1960's

Free vs Unfree Labor

  • In Free labor: the worker is
    • Legally entitled to the “fruits of his labor”
    • Working inside a form of contract also binding his employer
    • Able to relocate geographically or return home
  • A worker is unfree when any the above conditions is absent, and the worker no longer possesses agency
  • The history of the American South from 1865 to the mid 1960s is fundamentally the history of attempts to retain African-Americans in various forms of unfree labor

Definitions of Categories of Rights

  • Political Rights (a very explicit category)
    • A “vertical axis:” individual rights vis-à-vis various levels of gov’t
    • Secured by law, usually in a constitution
    • Examples: First Amendment rights, the right to vote, etc.
  • Social Rights (a much fuzzier category)
    • A “horizontal axis:” individual rights vis-à-vis other citizens
    • Not secured by law; protected by custom and community norms
  • Civil Rights (to make explicit that which had been fuzzy)
    • Non-political social rights so important they must be secured by law
    • 14th Amendment intended to grant limited civil rights to freedmen
    • Disputes over conflicting civil rights must be adjudicated in the courts

Amendment XIII of 1865

  • Section 1
    • “Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as punishment for crime… shall exist… within the United States”
  • Section 2
    • “Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation”
  • 1865-6 - Several Southern states pass “Black Codes” to circumvent, triggering Radical Reconstruction
  • Later circumvented by the “convict lease system,” widespread in the South during “Jim Crow” from the 1890s to the 1930s
  • Not effective because unfree labor still exists in the form of chain gangs

Overview Post-1865 Trans-Mississippi Expansion

  • American West is sparsely populated
  • Importance of the transcontinental railroads
    • Impetus for an expanded iron and steel industry (many “spinoffs”)
    • Railroads require many resources - akin to today’s SpaceX
      • Railroad bonds
      • Army engineers map broad railway channels
      • Fed only requires RR companies to purchase every other sq mile
    • An “integrated, national mass market” for mfg goods
    • Nation market is prerequisite for an international one
    • Factories can sell far and wide
  • First the railroads are built, then the farms and towns emerge
  • Western homesteading and the “20-inch rainfall line”
    • The Homestead Act of 1862
      • Applies to individuals, not (railroad) companies
      • Fed gives land to citizens free of charge
      • Before Act, land cost ~$1/acre
      • Not explicitly veteran program, but understood as one
      • Was not gendered - women could purchase, but rarely did
      • Now called BLM land
    • Homesteading, 1865-1885, higher rainfall, milder winters
    • Homesteading, 1885-1900, lower rainfall, severe winters
    • Low agricultural commodity prices and the cycle of debt
    • Average of 20 inches of rainfall west of rainfall line
    • Average of < 20 inches of rainfall east of line
    • ~80% of homesteaders return home
  • Role of cattle ranching and mining in Western development
    • Cattle drive - herd of cattle moved north from Texas to Chicago
    • Before civil war, most protein came from pork and chicken
    • Typical use of cows before civil war
      • One female cow for milk
      • One steer (castrated bull) to pull the plow
    • During Civil War, beef came into the forefront of the American mind
      • Rations for Union soldiers sometimes cut-off by the South
      • This forced Union soldiers to occasionally butcher cattle
    • Mining
      • Two types of metal
        • Precious
        • Monetary
        • Gold and silver
        • Money secured by the value of the metal
        • Risk of hyperinflation - overabundance of currency
      • Non-precious
        • Industrial
        • Electrical infrastructure increases demand for copper

Development of the Pacific Slope to 1890

  • By 1890 three centers of urbanization had emerged along the Pacific Slope:
    1. Northern California: San Francisco-Sacramento-San Joaquin Valley
    2. Willamette Valley-Seattle in Oregon and Washington
    3. Southern California
  • The West Coast was ethnically very diverse, but unhappily, with massive expropriation of Mexican land grants and fierce anti-Chinese agitation from labor unions resulting in the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882.
  • Agriculturally, California began to shift from wheat to fruit production after 1890 .

1877 - A Snapshot

  • The U.S. is poised to become a mature industrial society, the second after Great Britain. This will require the following
    • New concentrations of capital, to invest in expanded factory plant
    • New manufacturing technologies to exploit economies of scale
    • New sources of semi-skilled factory labor
    • Britain invests surplus capital, which pays off later in WWII
  • The U.S. is about to receive its second great influx of foreign immigration, this time from Southern and Eastern Europe
  • The U.S. is about to experience its second great pulsation of urbanization in the Northeast, Ohio Valley, and Great Lakes
  • The U.S. is about to experience accelerated income inequality
  • (The U.S. will not become a “world power” for 40 more years)
